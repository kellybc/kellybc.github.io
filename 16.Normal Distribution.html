16.Normal Distribution

The normal, or Gaussian, distribution is the most common distribution that is seen in statistics. The name Gaussian comes from Carl Friedrich Gauss. A bell curve is another common name for the distribution, although many different distributions resemble a bell shape. The distribution describes the behavior of a continuous random variable. The random variable is one where the variation is due entirely to random factors. There is an equal probability that there will be positive and negative deviations about the random variable's mean value. The distribution function is described by three parameters; the random variable $ x $, the population mean $ \mu $, and the population standard deviation $ \sigma $. The functional form of the distribution is

$$ P\left( x, \mu, \sigma \right) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2 \pi}} e^{\frac{-\left(x-\mu \right)^2}{2\sigma^2}} $$

This expression, known as the probability density function or PDF, is the equation of the curve you typically think of when you picture a bell curve. If the function is plotted against the random variable $ x $, the mean and standard deviation effects may be realized. Let's first look at the impact of the standard deviation. Shown in Figure 1 is a plot of three normal distributions with different standard deviations.

Figure 1: Normal distributions with different $ \sigma $'s

Understanding that standard deviation represents how dispersed a data set is, it can be reasoned that the tall plot (the blue one) has a smaller standard deviation than the other two plots. Indeed this is true. By the same line of reasoning, it may be seen that the plot that is spread out the most (the red one) has a larger standard deviation than the other two plots. If you think of the curve as a hill, then the standard deviation determines the hill's steepness. Figure 2 is a plot of three normal distributions with different mean values.

Figure 2: Normal distributions with different $ \mu $'s

Here, the mean value of the distributions affects the placement of the peak value. The peak of the distribution corresponds to the mean random variable value. This effect is understandable if the definition of the mean of a data set is considered. The mean of a data set represents the value that is most likely to occur. A distribution function describes the probability that a particular value occurs. Therefore the mean should appear at the peak value of the distribution function. Knowing that the mean value happens at the normal distribution's peak value, the mean may be read from the plot directly. For the distributions shown in Figure 2, the tallest distribution (the blue one) has a mean value of $0$. The green and red distributions have a mean value of approximately $5$ and $10$, respectively.

There is one last thing to note about these distribution functions. The $ y $ value of these curves at a particular $ x $ value represents the likelihood or probability that the specific $ x $ value will occur. The curve's integral must be taken to determine the probability that a particular $ x $ value will fall in a specific interval (say from $ a $ to $ b$). In other words, the probability that $ x $ will fall between $ a $ and $ b $ is given by

$$ P \left(a \le x \le b \right) = \int_a^b P\left(x\right) dx$$

where $ P\left(x\right) $ is the PDF. The expression above applies to all continuous random variable distributions, not just the normal distribution. If the interval is from $ - \infty $ to $ \infty $, then the probability is $ 1 $ for any continuous random variable distribution. In other words,

$$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P \left( x \right) dx = 1 $$

If the meaning of the PDF is considered, this conclusion should be expected. The PDF represents the probability that a particular $ x $ value occurs. If all those probabilities are summed together (recall that is what an integral does), then the total has to be $ 1 $.

Performing the integral calculation every time the normal distribution is to be used is not very practical. It may be seen that the integral would be challenging to calculate, especially to calculate by hand. For this reason, tabulated values have been created. This table is called a standard normal distribution table because it represents a normal distribution with a mean of $ 0 $ and a standard deviation of $ 1 $. You may ask why a mean of $ 0 $ and a standard deviation of $ 1 $? As previously mentioned, the normal distribution function is dependent on three variables; the random variable $ x $, the population mean $ \mu $, and the population standard deviation $ \sigma $. These three variables could produce an infinite number of variations of the same distribution (because the mean and standard deviation can vary). Instead of creating a new table for every combination of $ \mu $ and $ \sigma $, a transformation is used. This transformation is what creates the standard normal distribution. The transformation is given by

$$ z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} $$

where $ z $ is now the continuous random variable. The transformation reduces the PDF to the form

$$ P\left( z \right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^\frac{-z^2}{2} $$

Before the standard normal table is used, one important note must be made. There are different variations of standard normal tables that you may come across in various texts. The different variations arise from what is tabulated in the table. Shown above in Table 1 is a diagram of a normal distribution with a shaded region. This shaded region indicates the values reported in the table. For Table 1, the values in the table (excluding the top row and the left-most column) represent the shaded region. Other tables that are presented elsewhere may have differently shaded areas. Some examples are shown below in Figure 3.

Figure 3: Standard Normal Distribution Table Variations

All of the different variations have their pros and cons, so a particular variation is not necessarily better. It just depends on the application and the information that is desired from the table.
