22.Sampling Rate

When recording a signal at specific times, we have no knowledge of the signal between each data point

Figure 1: Various signals with the same sampling rate

it is simply not enough to collect data from a signal at any given sampling rate, but the sampling must be fast enough to capture the important characteristics

This requirement is a consequence of discrete sampling of a continuous signal

sampling rate is usually specified as a frequency in hertz ($ Hz $), often denoted as $ f_s $

A varying time signal may be composed of multiple frequency components

The single-component of a signal with the highest frequency is noted as the maximum signal frequency and is often denoted as $ f_m $

A sampling rate must be greater than twice the maximum signal frequency

$$ f_s \gt 2 f_m $$

This expression comes from the Nyquist Theorem

the Nyquist theorem expression assumes an infinite amount of sampling time

various mathematical techniques may be used to reconstruct the original signal

The general procedure for analyzing signals is

  1. Use various filtering techniques to remove any unwanted higher-order frequency components from the original signal.
  1. Use a sampling rate according to the Nyquist Theorem expression.
  1. The resulting plots may appear to suggest higher-order frequencies. However, if filtering was appropriately conducted, any higher-order frequency greater than the maximum signal frequency may be disregarded.

Sampling Rate Demonstration

Aliasing

Aliases are false frequencies that may appear in data

Figure 2: Alias Signal

Aliasing occurs with the sampling frequency does not meet the Nyquist Theorem criteria

Sometimes under-sampling cannot be avoided

When the sampling frequency is not high enough, an estimated lowest alias frequency may be made

Figure 3: Folding Diagram

Aliasing Example

Given:

A maximum signal frequency is estimated to be $ 80 Hz $. A sampling frequency of $ 100 Hz $ is used.

Required:

Compute the lowest alias frequency using a folding diagram.

Solution:

$$ f_m = 80 Hz $$
$$ f_s = 100 Hz $$
$$ f_N = \frac{ f_s }{ 2 } = \frac{100 Hz}{2} = 50 Hz$$
$$ \frac{f_m}{f_N} = \frac{80 Hz}{50 Hz} = 1.6 $$
$$ 0.4 = \frac{f_a}{f_N} $$
$$ f_a = 0.4 f_N = 20 Hz $$
